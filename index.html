Teaser In a world where magic is memory spent, three orphaned siblings are the reincarnated gods who once died to save it — and they don’t remember why.
Back Cover On the world of Vi’talitas, magic is Animancy — the art of burning your own memories for power, paid not in mana but in the feeling you lose forever. When a tear in reality begins flooding the world with Stigma, a corruption that hollows souls into monsters, the only ones who can seal it are three siblings raised in a home for lost children: Omin, who traded her sight to perceive the unseen; Amoz, who devours the wicked to free their souls; and I’onnie, who hears the dead but is deaf to the living. They are the last incarnations of the fallen god-souls who lost the first war — and to win this one, they must Recall the lives they died living. An epic of memory, mercy, and the cost of power.
The Deeper Hook The Great Mother A’kana gave her children the gift of Anima — the power to know and tend a soul — and was betrayed twice: once by her brother A’thule, who forged Stigma to unmake creation, and once from within, when one of her own protectors corrupted the divine Eversouls in a war that broke the world. A’kana spent the last of herself sealing the wound, scattering her surviving children into mortal rebirth with their memories wiped. Generations later, the seal is failing. The three who must mend it — the Elloyd siblings — live as ordinary, distrusted Medians, casters who pull power from their own past lives at the price of greying away, memory by memory, toward the same Stigma they’re sworn to fight. As they uncover what they were, they inherit not just power but guilt: the betrayer was family, the war was kin against kin, and the mercy they deliver to the corrupted — releasing trapped souls back to the cycle — is the same mercy one sister once gave the brother she was forced to strike down. Eternal Trivium braids a coming-of-age, a slow-burn tragedy of reincarnated love, and a cosmology where the only cure for corruption is to return to where you began.
Title & Branding Locked Series / world title: Eternal Trivium (no “The”). Names the whole structure of reality — the three-Pillar cosmos governed by the Twelve Laws — so every story sits beneath it. Iconic shape (adjective + weighty noun), a canon term rather than a bolted-on name, clean and sayable, and it quietly encodes the “three” that organizes the world. Animancy is the magic within the world (its “Force”), not the title. Three-tier brand: world title → magic system → book subtitles (e.g. Eternal Trivium: Sea of Lights).

Genre & Themes Locked

Eternal Trivium is Mythic Afrofantasy — a character-driven secondary world with its own metaphysics, gods, and afterlife, drawn from an African-inspired mythic imagination. It is a Reincarnation Saga at its spine, carried by an Aetherpunk register where soul-energy powers both relic and machine, set against a Parallel World reborn from the heart of a fallen god. Its emotional key is one of Metaphysical Wonder — braiding a coming-of-age, a slow-burn romantic tragedy of reincarnated love, and the cosmic-horror edge of Stigma and the Veils. At its heart it is a story about memory, grief, mercy, and the cost of power.

Voice & representation LockedThe prose centers Black, PoC, and LGBTQ+ characters and perspectives as a core, non-incidental part of the world — their cultures, relationships, and interiority written with depth and dignity rather than as token presence. The book is Black-, PoC-, and queer-friendly by design, both in who its heroes are and in how their stories are told.

The Twelve Eternal Laws Locked

If the Trivium is the structure, the Twelve Laws are its constitution — the principles that maintain balance across the three dimensions (Mind, Body, Soul), four to each. They are taught as maxims a Median lives and trains by — practical wisdom and cosmic principle at once.

Who keeps them LockedThe Eversouls upheld the Twelve collectively — not one keeper per Law, but a shared upkeep, like a choir holding a chord. When the Body line fell and the Spirit line broke, no single Law snapped; the whole balance thinned for want of keepers. This is why the present world slips even though the Laws still stand.
Laws of the Mind
  • The Memory is the Source — Memory is what Anima draws from; the root of all casting.
  • Prepare the Mind, the Body will follow — mental discipline precedes physical power.
  • Emotion does not equate to fact — feeling is not truth; the Neurolist’s discipline and a rebuke of Stigma.
  • Sight is not truth — perception deceives. The Law Silas (Illusion) violates; echoed in the prophecy’s “Believe not what your plain eyes see.”
Laws of the Body
  • Action is always louder than thought — the Body’s counterweight to the Mind; the vessel speaks.
  • The vessel does not forget — memory lives in flesh as well as mind (pairs with “The Memory is the Source”).
  • Revere your flesh. Let it speak for you — the body as honest expression.
  • What is broken still serves — the flawed vessel is the truest; the doomed-born carry the strongest will. (The protagonists embodied.)
Laws of the Soul — in A’kana’s own words
  • The Living will Die — no incarnate state is permanent.
  • The Dead will Live — death is not an end; the dead return to living.
  • From Anima we Emerge; To Anima we Return — the conservation Law that Stigma violates by refusing return.
  • So thy Soul will always Endure — the soul persists. Comfort to the faithful, sentence to the damned (Silas endures; A’thule endures).

How the Laws function LockedOrdinary Animancy operates within the Laws. Bending or breaking a Law is the source of forbidden power and a doorway for Stigma. The corrupted don’t break assigned Laws — they fail the balance their dimension was meant to keep (the fallen Body line: Desire, Ego, Morality, Integrity unbalanced; Silas violating Sight is not truth).

The Immortal Fissure Locked renamed: was “Eternal Cataclysm”

A’thule, ruler of the Mind, came to covet his siblings’ contentment and the Omneity’s authority. He struck first at A’kana, believing her benevolence made her weak. A’thesis intervened and was destroyed defending her; his remains were cast adrift among the Pillars. Enraged, A’kana spent vast energy to tear A’thule from the Astroverse and seal him in a parallel plane just outside the three Pillars — the False Pillars — fortified with Aether.

Aether & the Aegis Grid Locked

Aether is the Force of Life — A’kana’s substance. With it she built the Aegis Grid: aetheric currents separating the Pillars so they never directly intersect (which would collapse the balance, one realm engulfing another). The Grid’s powerful rivers form Leylines; their crossings are Leypoints. The Aegis has two jobs: keep the dimensions apart, and keep A’thule sealed.

Design payoffLeypoints = natural high-stakes zones / dungeons / contested territory, where the membrane between dimensions thins.

The Ley — the Sea of Lights Locked

The vast in-between of prismatic light where all soulstuff is collected and transformed into hallowed energy — the rebirth cycle. Souls return here and re-emerge as mortals with only faint echoes of past lives. Anima itself emerges from the Ley.

The Six Veils Locked

The Sea of Lights is divided into Six Veils — concentric regions of the Spirit Realm, with Ne’haris at the center core, surrounded by the Veils. They are not separate afterlives but stages within the rebirth cycle: a soul passes through the Veils, then returns to the Ley to re-emerge. A soul’s state at death — peaceful, troubled, unrepentant — shapes which Veils it moves through.

The Six Veils, innermost concerns outward

Home of the Pantheons and the Blessed — the highest reach of the Spirit Realm. Holds the realm of Heaven.

Where those who complete their Wandering and find peace come to rest. Holds the realm of Nirvana.

Stages of the cycle LockedThe Veils are the terrain of the journey between lives: return to the Ley → move through the appropriate Veils → drawn back into rebirth (memory wiped, re-emerging anew). Whether a soul travels toward Awakening and Elevation or down into Blight and Anathema reflects what it carried at death.
The center has changed LockedNe’haris was formerly the center core of the Veils. After the Gloaming — the Ghost Wars’ second battle — and rebirth, Vi’talitas now holds that center.
Walking the Veils LockedThose who can travel the Veils — Veilwalkers (revised from “Lightwalkers”), Riftstalkers, Leywardens, and sometimes Realmwatchers — use Ether Vessels, HEAT-SR Machines, and gear such as L.W.V.E Suits to move through the Veils with safety and focus. Most Leypoints are found in the Veils of Wandering, Blight, and Anathema. The untrained risk having their souls ripped away and cast into the Sea, or suffering Leyveil Sickness.

A’kana’s Slumber, the Polestar & Ne’haris Locked

Weakened beyond repair, A’kana created a sanctuary deep in the Pneumaverse. Her incorporeal body became The Polestar — a core of raw, chaotic, radiant aether — which birthed the realm Ne’haris, haven to the Unborn, Unliving, and Unseen, the divine nucleus of the Pneumaverse. There she sleeps, beyond the reach of A’thule’s taint.

The Aether Crux — The Great Mother Tree Locked

A’kana’s sleeping essence is also a living World Tree. The Aether Crux (Anima Crux, Tree of Life) is the sentient remaining essence of A’kana of Many Souls — the source of Ne’haris and a system of webbed tendrils spanning the world. Mortals know her as Yggdrasil, the Kabbalah, the World Tree; to Ne’haris she is simply “Great Mother A’kana.” Beyond white — luminance, radiating living light; her bark and leaves make the finest healing remedies known.

  • Beacon of Anima: saturated with soul-energy, she flows Anima out through her roots into worlds beyond — lending influence to all worlds she connects to.
  • The Leylines are her roots: the Ley Lines are the Roots of A’kana, like blood vessels. A new world gets a root that feeds it Anima; the network connects all living worlds to the Soul Space, recycling energy for Balance. Sever a root or kill a world, and it withers back to the Soul Realm.
  • The Aurora: the Crux birthed the Northern Lights — a gateway into the Sea of Lights through which souls traverse.
  • Nine Rune-Carved Obelisks: rune-carved wards around the tree, one for each of the Nine Brands of Anima, representing the pathways of Matter, Anti-Matter, and Dark Matter.
Why A’thule is limited LockedA’kana is mother of all Anima — every pathway is hers to govern, even the Anima A’thule wields. This is why A’thule can use only a fraction of his full power: her hold over the Anima Domains throttles him. Even sleeping, the Great Mother keeps her hand on the betrayer’s strength.

The Eversouls — A’kana’s Children Locked

To defend the Pneumaverse, A’kana created the Eversouls — her children, instrumental to the founding of Ne’haris and protectors of its deepest secrets. They are a distinct tier of being: below the Paramounts but far above ordinary souls.

Eighteen, then seventeen LockedA’kana tailored eighteen Eversouls — but A’sai was never born (slain in incubation by Silas), leaving seventeen active. The Eversouls are not the Brands themselves; they are the smaller constituent parts of the nine Brands of Anima — finer Aspects of the Soul that, together, compose each Brand. So the nine Brands and the eighteen Eversouls are two scales of the same system: the Brand is the whole, the Eversouls its facets.

The Betrayal of Silas Locked

In the War for Ne’haris, many Eversouls were Stigmatized — through an inside betrayal by one of their own brothers, Silas. This is the cosmology’s second betrayal-by-kin, rhyming one tier down from A’thule: betrayal-by-family is the recurring engine from gods to children to mortals. It also resolves how Ne’haris was wounded — from within, by one of its protectors. With particular cruelty, Silas killed his sister A’sai in incubation, before she could be born (her status, Unborn, is literal) — yet she arrived anyway as I’onnie.

SoulCraft — the Eversouls’ Art Locked

The old name for Animancy LockedSoulCraft is the ancient name for Animancy. Before the present age, the Eversouls did not call their art “Animancy” — they called it SoulCraft. The two are the same discipline across two ages: what the Eversouls practiced as SoulCraft is what Medians now practice, in diminished form, as Animancy.

When A’kana made the Eversouls, she gifted each a power that amplifies the Aspect they embody — a SoulCraft: a personal style of anima-based attack and expression. To receive it, an Eversoul undergoes a ritual that bonds and infuses the craft into an object — their Animatrux, a living crystallized energy source that serves as the key power-source of that Eversoul, keeping their aura perpetually at arms.

  • SoulCrafts take elemental and conceptual forms — Shadowcraft, Flamecraft, Terracraft, Lightcraft, and many more.
  • Though mastered most fully by the Eversouls, SoulCraft is not theirs alone — beings across the Fields can learn it. One must first know which Pillar their Anima falls under, then train to hone the Anima a given craft requires.
Energy is a fluxEnergy flows; it cannot die — only transmute and return, trapped and freed at once. All energy issues from the Soul: souls create energy, and energy is soul. To draw out this ancient, primal energy at will is to bend Aether — soul-energy itself.

The Unresolved War Locked as ambiguous

A’thule’s forces warred to seize Ne’haris — and through it, A’kana’s essence and endless aether. How the war ended is deliberately unknown in-world: some say Ne’haris fell, some say it was saved, some say A’kana abandoned it. This ambiguity is a feature, not a gap.

Resolved LockedThe three are the Elloyd siblings of the Whispers of the Mother, each embodying one of Ne’haris’s trinity (Unborn / Unliving / Unseen) — not mirrors of the Paramounts.

Anima & Animancy

Anima
The engine, the Nine Brands, and soul access

Anima — the Source Locked

Anima is inherent and tied to the intensity of the soul. One soul lives many lives; each adds experience. The more lives lived, the more powerful the soul and the greater its base anima pool. Young souls have little; some have none.

Origin of Anima LockedAnima is the “magic” substance of the world — a natural element permeating the entire Pneumaverse, the stuff the Aegis Grid is made of and that A’kana’s aura radiates. It originated from A’kana as a gift: the means to navigate the existence and makeup of a Soul. So Animancy is not stolen or transgressive — it is a birthright of the Pneumaverse, the Great Mother’s endowment. (A sacred gift, not a theft.)
The Eye of the Aegis LockedAnima is produced at the Eye of the Aegis — the center and core of Vi’talitas, where the Great Mother Tree stands. From this core, Anima wells up and pushes outward through A’kana’s roots (the Leylines) into the Aegis Grid and the worlds beyond. Because Vi’talitas is Ne’haris, the Eye is the single point where the Tree, the world’s core, and the wellspring of all Anima coincide — the beating heart of the cosmos’s soul-economy.

Auric Remanence — the Eighth Sense Locked

Every race on Vi’talitas possesses Auric Remanence as a literal sense — alongside sight, sound, scent, taste, touch, movement, body-limit, and message-interpretation. It is the soul’s retained reservoir of past-life experience — the residue that remains after each life’s field has passed — and the ability to identify memories from past lives and call upon them for present use. It is the sensory faculty that underlies all memory-casting: Animancy is Auric Remanence, trained and spent.

How a soul finds its Brand LockedThrough Auric Remanence, the soul retains the emotional experiences that left the most defined impressions upon it across its many lifetimes. Some emotions are more deeply ingrained in a given soul than others — and through that dominant emotional register, the soul becomes associated with a particular Brand of Anima. The Feii are its master-teachers, their connection to the Nyph tying the elemental peoples to this lineage.

Casting — Recall & Release Locked

A Median casts by Recalling a memory (surfacing it through Auric Remanence until its charge is raw) and Releasing that charge through a Brand, which shapes it into an effect — simply Recall → Release. Memory intensity equals cast intensity. The memory is the fuel; the Brand is the lens (any memory can fuel any Brand). The cost is felt, not numeric: Releasing a memory means temporarily losing access to its emotional charge. The act is fundamentally internal — the Median reaches inward rather than relying on an external mechanism.

The visible signature — the Signets LockedCasting is subtractive, and the signature is the cost made visible. Where other powers add light to the world, Animancy briefly takes light from the caster. Every Median bears Signets — Anima-marks placed on the body at awakening, their location fixed by Brand so placement announces a Median’s discipline (Origin on the face, Qhaos on the back, etc.; see the Brand Resonance table). Aether is the exception, surfacing where the Median channels best. They lie dormant until casting.
The Cast Cycle
Recall → Release → Recovery — one visible cascade of color leaving and returning.
Recall
Color drains from the body and refocuses into the Signets and eyes, which ignite in the Brand’s color. The eyes take the Brand hue regardless of natural color: sclera and iris both flood with it, leaving only the pupil as a point of normal black or white (by race). The Median’s hair also shifts to the Brand’s color for the cast. No white remains in the eye — a striking, faintly inhuman look.
Release
The gathered charge fires through the Brand into the effect.
Recovery
Color bleeds back into the body; Signets and eyes cool to normal. Recovery speed = power — brief for a master, lingering for the weak or overdrawn.
Two tells, read at different moments LockedLoud-novice / quiet-master reads during Release: a novice leaks, the Signet-mark blazing wild; a master bleeds almost nothing, the mark a near-silent whisper. Drain-and-recovery reads after: a master is quick in the return; the weak stay greyed. The axes aren’t redundant — one reads in the firing, one in the aftermath. A true master is quiet in both.
Greying made visible LockedBecause casting is “color leaves, then returns,” a greyed memory is one whose color never fully comes back. Overspend, and a Signet stays dull, an eye stays washed-out — a permanent, readable scar. A veteran’s Signets map their history of loss; a sharp onlooker can read how much a Median has spent of themselves off their own body.
Omin — the signature in full Locked

The Aetherseer casts with the full cycle — drain, Signets and eyes alight in Aether’s gold, recovery. The poignancy of how a sightless caster’s lit eyes read — what it means to blaze with a light she cannot see — is reserved for her character pass.

The Sequence — The Recall State Locked

Casting is not a single instantaneous act but an entered state that can then be spent from repeatedly — like a fighter’s powered-up form.

Ignite → Sustain → Release (repeatable)
Recall is ignition
Entering the charged state can be near-instant for the skilled — a master drops into Recall in a heartbeat; a novice is slow. Speed of entry is itself a measure of skill.
A sustained form
Once in the Recall State, the Median holds it — it persists for as long as their soul-stamina allows.
Release doesn’t end it
Firing a Release does not drop them out of Recall. They stay in the form and can Release as often as required while it holds — a barrage, not a single shot.
Savvy is the fuel
What limits the form is Savvy (soul-stamina). Each Release and moment held draws on it; when it runs low the form dulls and collapses. (See Vitalitas → Savvy, Game System.)
Endurance is trained
Holding the form longer and recovering Savvy faster is a discipline every Median must build — even masters worked to extend it. Soul age helps; endurance is earned.
Why it matters LockedThis turns casting from a per-shot transaction into a resource-managed state — a Median chooses when to enter the form, then fights from inside it until their Savvy gives out. Combat gains a rhythm: ignite → barrage → burn down → recover. Soul-stamina, not per-cast cost, is the real ceiling on power in a fight.

The Memory — Emotion Shapes the Cast Locked

A memory is not fuel of one flat kind. Every memory carries an emotion or sensation, and that feeling shapes the cast. Intensity of memory still sets intensity of effect — but the character of the feeling steers how that power expresses.

  • Tendencies, not a table. A memory invoking anger tends to increase intensity (force); one invoking calm tends to increase precision (control). Other feelings carry their own leanings — but two angry memories never produce identical spells. (Real rules, human cost, never arithmetic.)
  • Casting is autobiographical. The feeling is the steering, so a Median’s magic reflects who they have been. A caster who fights well on rage is a different person from one who fights on grief or joy.
  • Vs. Vitalitas: attributes are the baseline; emotion is the in-the-moment modulation. Tenacity sets how hard you can hit; casting on anger is how you lean into it now. They stack.
The core tension LockedThe most emotionally intense memories are the most powerful fuel and the most dangerous (see Mania). The best Medians aren’t those with the strongest memories — they’re those who can ride strong memories without drowning.

The Mania — Losing Oneself to the Memory Locked

Because casting means feeling, a Median can Recall too much — pulling a memory so deep it stops being a tool and becomes a place. They tip into Mania (distinct from the Berserker Role). It has two faces, and the memory’s emotion decides which strikes:

The two faces of Mania
Outward — loss of control
Memories that throw the cast outward (rage → force) throw the Median outward: the Brand fires wild and uncommanded, no aim, no off-switch — a danger to allies and self. The body acts; the steering is gone.
Inward — loss of self
Memories that turn the cast inward (grief, calm) pull the Median inward: consciousness submerges into the memory, leaving a coma-like state — present in body, absent in mind, trapped reliving it. They must find their way out.
The two bulwarks LockedTwo Vitalitas attributes guard the two faces. Savvy holds the form (run out, the Recall State collapses); Actuality — perceive and assert what is real — anchors the self (run low, the memory swallows you). A Median can fail two ways: burn out (Savvy) or lose themselves (Actuality).
Getting out LockedMild mania: the Median claws their own way out. Dire mania: a Soothsayer can reach in and guide the lost back — a vital role in combat and aftercare.
The lasting mark LockedNot a fixed penalty. Sometimes a memory is permanently altered or erased. Sometimes the Median loses several memories of the same emotion — a whole register drained at once (burn through grief, lose grief itself). Sometimes the cost is time — meditation and therapy to heal. The “lose a whole emotion” outcome is a character arc in a mechanic, and a road toward what Silas became.
Silas contrast LockedMania is the inverse of Silas. He is fully Stigmatized yet self-possessed — the one being who doesn’t lose himself. A Median lost to Mania is what it looks like to drown in the very thing Silas mastered.

Recovery & Greying Locked

  • Renewable by default — charge is exhausted, not destroyed. Rest restores it.
  • Greying — drawing too deep, too fast, or too often on one memory keeps the facts but loses the feeling, then eventually the memory itself.
  • Permanent loss — overspending past what rest can recover.
  • Old souls rotate through many memories and last longer; young souls grey fast. This is the stamina curve and the power hierarchy in one mechanic.
Emotional weightMemory is the only inheritance that survives death (if you stay out of the Ley). To spend memory is to spend your immortality.

Society on Vi’talitas

How the Present Age Began — Enter the Ley Locked

For generations, Vi’talitas was an ordinary world — mystical races coexisting, anima-rich but stable. Then its fabric gave way. A massive rift tore open, entangling Vi’talitas with what lay beyond, and Stigma poured through — a corruptive miasma that burns the eyes and mouth, lethal by exposure. Those who gave in became Stigmants, flooding the world as the rift widened, consuming or corrupting people, beasts, plants, even the raw elements.

At that darkest hour, the Medians awakened. Dormant anima rose across every region; past-life memories flooded back into the population, and the Polestar — nucleus of the Ley — awakened, and its people with it. Vi’talitas began returning to what its people had only read of in ancient tomes.

One group of Medians sealed the great breach, saving the reborn world at the cost of their anonymity — remembered only as Type Genesis, the first to show how far Animancy could grow. Their research confirmed Vi’talitas was once Ne’haris. Generations on, this is legend.

Today, Stigmants are a constant threat but no longer apocalyptic. Rifts still open; now there are Medians to subdue them and Generics to support. This is the world the series opens in.

Type Genesis LockedThe nameless first Medians who sealed the original breach, proved Animancy’s potential, and confirmed Ne’haris itself to be a Vitalitas — a living nucleus. A ready legend, a possible secret lineage, and the root of the “Type” naming.

Medians in Society Locked

Medians are now essential to Vi’talitas — but it wasn’t always so. For ages they were feared, ridiculed, condemned, and distrusted. Many Generics exploited young Medians through the Undertrade Exchange, a black market that stole newly orphaned Median children with untapped potential; the unluckiest never died clean deaths, and those who fought to live carried an unshakeable will. The arrival of Stigma changed much — reframing Medians from threat to necessity. Even now, an uncontrolled Median is treated as a liability — managed risk, not fear or worship.

HookThe Undertrade Exchange — trafficking in stolen Median children — is a ready source of villains, backstory wounds, and rescue/origin arcs.

Median Types — Archetypes Locked

Each Median’s proficiency identity resonates with one brand-group. A Median finds their Type as they mature.

Neurolist
Mind Preservation — “if you can think it, it can happen,” taken literally. Second most common.

Emotion is strongly tethered to their psionic power — constant emotional control is key. They fill education, governance, technology, and biological wellness.

Physiolist
Vessel Deconstruction — enhanced physical prowess. The most common Type.

Strength, speed, sight — sometimes extra limbs. Their gifts often manifest visibly in the body, the most distinctive Type to the eye. They fill agriculture, manufacturing, infrastructure, and environmental stewardship.

Vitalist
Soul Restoration — deep connection to the soul. The rarest Type.

Uncannily natural command of Anima; they commune with the Living and Unliving alike and attune to the very material of the Ley.

The Vitalist’s Sacrifice Locked As they grow in skill, a Vitalist retracts one of the five senses (sight, sound, touch, taste, or scent) — deeper attunement may cost more than one. The personal price of the rarest gift, and a vivid character-design lever. In exchange, they have flexibility of technique the other Types lack.
On “born” MediansA Median’s essence is infused with Anima, awakening in adolescence (timing varies by race). This fits the soul-intensity premise: a soul intense enough carries enough Anima to awaken and infuse the vessel; Generics lack that intensity. The name Median comes from their eventual ability to cross the rifts into the Sea of Lights — they are “in the middle” of worlds.
Alternate awakening LockedUnder the right circumstances, severe trauma can awaken a Median’s power early — even in infancy — bypassing normal adolescent timing. The Elloyd siblings awakened this way.

After training, every Median must take a Service — your class/career archetypes for the RPG.

Why “Service” Locked · NewMedians are a distrusted minority — capable, and far outnumbered by Generics. To be permitted to train and wield such power, a Median must be seen to be useful and accountable to society and the wider Pneumaverse. So the professions are framed as Services: sanctioned roles that make a Median’s power legible and “trusted.” Choosing a Service is not optional — being registered to one is how a Median is allowed to exist openly. What is optional is the organization: a Lawseeker need not join the Regency, but they must still be listed as a Lawseeker. The architecture of corruption

A’thule’s forces form a military hierarchy descending from the sealed god to the individual soul.

  1. A’thule — Father of Corruption (the sovereign). Sealed Mind-Paramount of Many Faces. Cannot act directly; commands through his elite. Seduction, not force — he convinces.
  2. The Falseones (the Generals). A’thule’s children made of pure Stigma — the anti-Eversouls, each built to combat a specific Eversoul. Grown in the False Pillars — the “Cold-Bloods”: Advisor Null “Malice,” General Vaine “Grinder,” the Sons of Animosity, the Daughters of Deprivation. Two (Null & Vaine) defect. Arc villains / boss tier — full nature below.
  3. The Grey Makers (the Lieutenants). Field officers who reach into the world — Silas’s creations, answering to him. Their core is the stigmatized Eversouls Silas corrupted (turned against their own home); the ranks grew with other fallen souls since. They “wear familiar faces” because the founding Grey Makers are family.
  4. ↳ Silas sits in his own tier beside the ladder — he commands a unique private unit no one else does: the turned Eversouls (the Grey Makers). Above the Lieutenants (they’re his); denied the Generals’ tier. (See his card.)
  5. Stigmants (the infantry). Mindless products of the spread, pouring from rifts (§4).

The Elysian Institute Locked · New

Another Median training school — a rival to the A.A.A., but smaller, stricter, and far more selective about whom it deigns to train. Where Triple-A is a home, Elysian is a proving ground: prestigious, demanding, and openly elitist. Its graduates are formidable and often rise to high-ranking positions across the organizations — but they carry a reputation for looking down on Medians and Generics alike, and are less-liked for it. To wear an Elysian education is to be respected and resented in the same breath.

Design payoffA class/prestige axis among Medians: A.A.A. (communal, ethical) vs. Elysian (elite, cold). Instant social shorthand — where a character trained signals values and standing before they speak. Fertile for rivalry, snobbery, and the “talented outsider vs. polished insider” dynamic.

The Aggregate Regency Locked · New

The primary governing body of Vi’talitas — the spanning order of the Vitae Age, risen after the Ghost Wars. Its members are deeply schooled in the Rules of the Trivium, the histories of Ne’haris and Vi’talitas, and the mythology of all that came before. A prominent presence that supersedes all other organizations — the flagship of the Lawseeker Service, and the authority the other orders answer to. Called “the Regency” for short.

In the place of the Eversouls Locked · NewThe name is literal: a regency rules in a sovereign’s absence. The Regency governs in the place of the Eversouls — and it governs on the settled belief that they are never returning: all Greyed, or lost under A’thule’s control. This is the premise the whole body is built on.
Latent irony — for the writerThe series treats the siblings as the Eversouls’ renewed defense, with sleeping Eversouls still to be found. So the government of Vi’talitas rules on a premise the protagonists exist to disprove. A regent who has declared the throne empty forever has every reason to keep it empty — not evil, but invested in a comfortable certainty. Another institution that decided a truth was too costly to hold open: the Regency withholds hope. Keep this latent; it’s a lever, not yet a plot beat.

Legitimacy flows from the eldest, most cosmically-placed people alive: the same Umbran Council that sits in the Unseen order (§3) leads the Regency’s governing body. Who better to declare the sovereigns gone than the oldest race to remember them?

The Company of Guardians Locked · New

The primary flagship of the Realmwatcher Service — and the one organization directly employed by the Regency. If the Realmwatchers are Vi’talitas’s second-largest Service and its shield, the Company is its standing army from within: a protective force spread across the land, stalwart defenders of the people. Realmwatchers serve as both a military force and a lawkeeping authority — which role a given Guardian fills depends on how they trained and where they are stationed: a border posting leans military, a settlement posting leans lawkeeping. Either way they are trained to ensure the safety and security of the people, and bound by strict codes of regulation in how they may act. Without the Company’s Guardians, much of the commonfolk would go undefended. Many of its Medians practice Animancy as Physiolists — the Body discipline suiting a defensive line.

Required, if not beloved Locked · NewRealmwatchers are not always liked — a visible authority with the power to police is rarely warmly received — yet they are required in countless ways, and Vi’talitas leans on them heavily. It is the strict code that keeps that authority legitimate; where the code holds, the Guardians are the reason a region sleeps safe.
The shield has a shadow Locked · NewA military force answering to the government is only as just as its officers. Some regions have suffered oppression under the Company — not by design, but through greedy leaders who turned a protective mandate into a heavy hand. The Guardians defend the people; they have also, in places, been the thing the people needed defending from.

The Bureau of Bastards Locked · New

An Oathbound talent agency — the flagship for hired expertise of every stripe. Oathbound enlist here to take on odd jobs they alone can complete with fine skill, and the Bureau brokers them out. It takes both lawful and unlawful work: for the Bureau, it is only ever about the money. A single roster might run from a stonemason to a bounty hunter to an assassin.

Code, not chaos — the line from the Undertrade Locked · NewWhat separates the Bureau from the Undertrade is a moral code. The Bureau is registered, rule-bound, and self-policing — its Oathbound are properly Serviced, its contracts have limits, and it answers, at arm’s length, for its people. The Regency tolerates it precisely because it polices itself: a regulated grey market is a known quantity. The Undertrade has no code at all — it traffics stolen children and owns no such line. Grey, then, but not lawless: the Bureau will take dark work; it will not take any work.

The Avocation Commission Locked · New

The lawful-only Oathbound flagship — and the Bureau’s clean mirror. The Commission employs Oathbound who support the communities they live in, with branches all across Vi’talitas. Its people are honorable, hardworking, and civilian by nature: no violent work, no grey contracts. Where the Bureau sells expertise to the highest bidder, the Commission spends it on the common good.

Design payoffTwo Oathbound flagships give the Service a full moral spectrum under one profession: Commission (civic, clean) → Bureau (grey, coded) → Undertrade (lawless, coded-less). A character’s employer places them on that spectrum instantly — and lets an Oathbound cross between them as a character arc.

Abbeys of the Aspects Locked · New

The Anitech Service has no housing organization — Anitechs are independent by nature, and no single body holds them all. What the Service does require is training, and that is the Abbeys’ purpose: not employers, but a network of training grounds where a craftsperson prepares for the pilgrimage that makes a true Anitech. Each Abbey blends mechanical training with sacred belief, readying both mind and skill for the work — and each is devoted to a particular Aspect as its focal point (some to Genos, others to the remaining Aspects), so an Abbey’s character reflects the Aspect it serves. There are many Abbeys across Vi’talitas.

The one Service open to both Locked · NewAnitech is the only Service that accepts both Median and Generic. The reason is structural: the other Services channel an innate caster discipline a Generic cannot wield — but Anitech is a learned craft, a pilgrimage and a skill, not a casting. A Generic can be taught the sacred work even if they can never cast it. This makes the Abbeys the one training ground where Median and Generic sit side by side as equals in the same calling. (This is a deliberate exception to the Median-only framing of the Services — §7.5.)
Respectability & the blacklisted Locked · NewTo become an Anitech is a respectable position. That respect is exactly what makes its loss so heavy: the blacklisted become taboo to work with, cut off from Aspect-sanctioned materials and reputable trade alike. This is the road to the Underarch (§5) — a lapsed Anitech, barred from honest craft, forging illicit relics from stolen and dying Matrux.

The Undertrade Exchange Locked

A black market that stole newly orphaned Median children with untapped potential; the unluckiest never died clean deaths, and those who fought to live carried an unshakeable will. The arrival of Stigma reframed Medians from threat to necessity — but the Exchange’s shadow persists. Beyond trafficking, it has been known to use children in its employ to source Matrux material, since a Median child is most likely to find it without having to be an Anitech.

Vast, and beyond the Regency’s grip Locked · NewThe Undertrade is incredibly vast — so sprawling that the Regency has long struggled to bring it under control. It is run by its own small council, yet its reach draws in Medians and Generics of every kind. Unlike the Bureau of Bastards, it holds to effectively no moral code — most of its people are untrustworthy by nature, and what little order it keeps serves profit, not principle.
The trades of the Exchange Locked · NewIts crimes run wide: child Median trafficking and child labor (still among its primary income), drug import and export, Underarch creation, and other Stigma-based variants.
“Grey Matter.” Its most notorious product — a hallucinogenic drug that causes a Median to temporarily “grey out”, losing themselves in a pleasurable memory to the point of intoxication. It has boomed across many spaces; highly dangerous, and yet another of the Exchange’s machinations the Regency has failed to stamp out.
Design note — Grey MatterThe drug weaponizes the cosmology’s own spine: greying is the sacred cost of spending Auric Remanence (identity spent to cast), and to chase that greying for pleasure is a profane inversion — addiction as self-erasure by choice. It rhymes darkly with “saving is killing” and the Grey Makers: here a Median pays their immortality for a high.

Locked this pass — see Timeline ArchiveAn Aeon holds 18 Waves; a Blink holds 3,000 Lilliblinks. One Vitae reckoning year is lived as a year yet spans ~1,000 Earth-equivalent years (hence kya dates; present day = 326 kya). The year runs three Seasons of six Waves — Lull, Eld, Vim — Waves named for the Eversouls, Beams for the Divine Aspects. Full system in the Timeline Archive.

Two scales measure the same passing time: Earth time (the mortal realm’s ruler) and Pneuma time (the Spirit Realm’s). They sit side by side for one purpose — to dramatize how staggeringly old most souls are. A mortal life is brief on the Earth scale; counted in Pneuma units, a soul’s existence stretches almost beyond comprehension.

The felt comparison LockedThe two are not a precise conversion — the equivalences are felt, not arithmetic (in keeping with “the Between”). A mortal century feels like a Pneuma Novam; a mortal hour feels like a Pneuma Aura. The point is the gap between the rulers: a long life in Earth years reads as a vast, deep age in Pneuma units — which is why an old soul carries such weight (its depth feeds its anima pool, instinct, and power; see §4).

The One-Sentence Spine

Three estranged adult siblings — the renewed forms of A'kana's intended defense — are forced back together when the most reckless of them vanishes following a voice from a life he can't remember, and their search reopens the murder that orphaned them, leading all three toward the brother who has hunted them across lifetimes to keep one prophecy from ever being assembled.

Not “stop Silas and free everyone” — that is the series. Book one is the moment the personal loss and the cosmic loss are revealed as the same loss. The cosmic objectives are the occasions; the subject is the price these three specific people pay.

◆The Prophecy — Locked

The keystone decision that closes the architecture.

The prophecy is not a projection of victory — it is instruction. It tells the siblings how to defeat Silas and A'thule, not that they will. It is conditional, actionable, and must be enacted at a cost.

  • SilasOnly Silas holds it whole. This is why he hunts them: not fear of fate, but fear of information they could assemble. You can kill the merely fated; you cannot un-know a completed instruction. So it must never complete — kill them before they gather it, across lifetimes. This is why he tracks reincarnation.
  • All threeThe instruction is distributed across exactly these three souls by design. It can only be assembled by the three people it is written into — which is why A'kana needed all three renewed, why Silas needs all three dead, and why the estranged trio is structurally irreplaceable to one another.
  • OminReaches the part buried at the soul/Ley boundary — earned through the grief of finally understanding what she heard that night. Not acquired; suffered into.
  • AmozReaches the part that must be walked toward through danger — learned from the inside, at the risk of becoming what the prophecy warns against.
  • IonReaches the part that can only be read — accessible precisely because of the wound of being the one Silas killed first.
The fragments are not collectible objects — that is the youth-grammar trap in disguise. Each is a truth that costs something to integrate and changes the sibling who receives it. Test: does “finding a part” read like picking up an item, or surviving a revelation? It must always be the second.
Assembling the instruction is also the moment they learn what defeating Silas will cost them — method implies price. Completion should not feel like “victory unlocked” but like finally understanding the terms, and the terms should frighten them. Possible: the tragic Silas who believes he is sparing them that cost — the dark mirror of the family's protective-withholding instinct.

◆The Cast at Book Open

Omin (D'vita, reborn) · Primary POV. Eldest, the shield. Sightless; exceptional hearing drives her Animancy; hand-to-hand. Leywarden by choice, seeking why they were orphaned. The only living sibling who remembers the night. Distance is how she protects — she made the childhood pattern permanent by choosing the lonely Service.
Amoz (I'nácio, transformed) · The absence that drives book one. Lightwalker. Recklessness revealed as recognition — he follows a voice from his I'nácio life. Missing ~1 year at open.
Ion (A'sai, the Unborn) · Soothsayer. A baby the night their parents died — no memory of it. Maisel's sole confidante. The reconciler between two damaged witnesses.
Maisel (Mulu'lizi) · Found them as teens. Formerly Stigmatized; memories fracturing and resurfacing against her will. A protector whose protection may have been helping them not-remember.
Silas (Emerald Essence of Illusion) · Betrayer-brother. Tracked their reincarnation; struck at their parents to abort it. Behind everything — but must not read as omniscient. He has failed before. He escalates because he is afraid.

IThe Comfortable Story

Establish the three separated lives, then break the thing that kept them apart.

  • OminOpen on Omin mid-Leywarden work, alone. Establish her by action and sound — sightless navigation, the trouble that finds her, the weight she carries silently. Not by exposition.
  • OminThe separation is shown as normal: occasional contact, duties pulling them apart, Amoz's long absences treated as routine. The reader should feel the comfortable lie before it breaks — “he always comes back.”
  • AmozThe break: something makes this absence different.
  • Omin · IonOmin and Ion converge — reluctantly, friction of estrangement intact. They do not slot easily back together. Years did something to them.
What is the concrete trigger? Something Amoz would never miss? A trail gone cold where it shouldn't? Whatever it is, it should implicate the sisters' complacency — they let the comfortable explanation hold a few months too long. That retroactive guilt is free fuel.
End of Movement I — the turnThe decision to actually look for Amoz. Not heroic — guilty.

IITwo Searches, One Thread

Run the personal search and the cosmic search in parallel — without the characters yet knowing they are the same.

  • AmozThe trail pulls forward — toward the voice, toward something pre-Elloyd. The reader's lore-knowledge primes dread: this voice reads like a Stigma lure even if it isn't.
  • OminThe search for Amoz reactivates her search for that night. Half-heard memories surface — her mother's voice, her stories of “what their people believed,” the Fonixi / Ra'gnn fragments. She navigates the past as she navigates space: by sound, by what she heard but never saw.
  • Maisel · IonMaisel's Soothsaying yields pieces she can share only with Ion. Her fractured, formerly-Stigmatized memory delivers exposition compromised — never clean, never fully trustworthy.
  • Cost plantedFirst terrible Grey Maker encounter seeds here. Establish the price early: to cleanse Stigma is to erase identity. Saving is killing.
Amoz's voice — corruption, something the cosmology has taught everyone to fear as corruption, or genuinely ambiguous? Keep it ambiguous to the end of book one. The question is not “what is it” but “is Amoz still Amoz when they reach him.”
Maisel's memory loss must have rules. Stigma cleanses by returning soul-identity to the Ley — so her fractures aren't random amnesia, they are partial soul-erasure. What she's lost vs. retained should map the shape of the threat. The absence is information — decide the rule now, or it becomes a writer's escape hatch.
End of Movement II — the turnA piece of Amoz's trail and a piece of Omin's memory rhyme — the first hint, felt before understood, that the voice and the night come from the same source.

IIIConvergence

The longest, hardest movement. Two searches collapse into one. The spine made visible. Build this first — if it holds, the rest follows.

  • RealizationThe sisters realize they have been investigating the same event from opposite ends of memory: Amoz pulled forward toward a past he can't remember; Omin digging backward into a past she remembers too well.
  • OminMaiselThe two recovered memories collide — and may not match. Two protectors, same instinct (bury what hurts the family), in direct conflict over whether the truth should surface. Omin may discover Maisel did to her exactly what Omin has always done to Amoz and Ion.
  • SilasThe realization about Silas arrives as betrayal understood, not mystery solved. He killed A'sai in incubation → Ion is the Unborn because of him. He is upstream of what each of them is. Land it on the body, not the lore.
  • FragmentsEach sibling's distributed part of the instruction begins to surface — bound to their nature and Service. The prophecy is glimpsed, never whole, because no one but Silas holds it complete.
The reunion is not a rescue. Three options for the cliffhanger that defines the series register: (a) found but changed/changing; (b) found but having chosen the voice with open eyes — agent, not victim; (c) not found, but his fate now understood. Recommendation: (b), with the cost ambiguous. It makes him tragic and willed — a mirror of every soul called home to the Ley.
End of Movement III — the turnThey reach Amoz, or where Amoz is. The personal wound and the cosmic wound are now one wound — and the siblings can see it.

IVThe Question Made Visible

Resolve book one's plot (the Amoz search) while opening the series' true subject.

  • AmozThe immediate thread resolves enough to satisfy book one — at a cost that reframes everything.
  • The trioThe three end changed in relation to each other. The blood tie survives what time did to them — or survives barely, which is more honest.
  • The warThe full shape becomes visible: murdered parents, the Unborn, the transformed, the witness, Silas, the corrupted predecessors, the sleeping Eversouls still to find — all one continuous campaign. They now understand the war they're in.
  • ApertureThe series question — rescue or severance, salvation or long goodbye — is now askable. Book one earns the right to ask it. It does not answer it.
Final beat — strongest candidates: (a) the first Grey Maker sibling identified, making “saving is killing” personal and imminent; (c) Silas seen afraid for the first time, confirming he escalates because he is losing. Recommendation: combine (a) and (c) — the personal cost ahead, and the enemy revealed as frightened rather than omnipotent.

The Theme That Unifies It

Every figure of love or authority in these siblings' lives has decided the truth was too costly to hand over. The series is about what withholding does to the people you withhold from — and whether love that protects by hiding is love, or a quieter kind of harm.

Keep the four withholdings irreconcilable — tender, negligent, sacrificial, and monstrous — so the theme stays alive instead of flattening into one note struck four times.

OminShields by taking the weight in silence.
MaiselShielded by helping them not-remember.
SilasWithholds the prophecy — believing, perhaps, he spares them its cost.
The parentsDied protecting them — the founding protection-through-removal.

◆Cross-Cutting Pressure Tests

  1. The collection-quest trap. Freeing Grey Makers / finding Eversouls / stopping Silas is structurally a gather-the-pieces quest — youth grammar. Defense: every objective must hurt because of who these people are. Each Grey Maker freed = family chosen to be erased.
  2. Silas omniscience. He tracks reincarnation across lifetimes — edges toward a villain who puts the heroes on rails. Defense: he has failed before. Escalation = fear, not godhood. A frightened brother running out of chances is more dangerous and more interesting.
  3. Maisel as escape hatch. “She misremembered” can paper over anything. Defense: fixed rules for what fractures vs. holds, tied to Stigma-as-soul-erasure. The absence must be information.
  4. Omin under-rendered. She is the primary but was developed last. Defense: put weight on her on the page. She carries the series because she carries the memory — don't let the primary become the structural orphan of her own book. She orphaned herself (chosen distance) the way the others were orphaned.
  5. The voice as lure. Your own lore primes the reader to read it as Stigma. Use that — don't resolve it too early. Ambiguity is the tension.
  6. Three siblings, one unit? Adult trios carry divergence. Make their occasional unity earned against three Services and three lives — not assumed, which would be ensemble-cohort youth grammar leaking back in.

◆Open Questions, Ranked by Draft-Leverage

  1. Resolved ✓ — keep developingProphecy: instruction, not projection; held whole only by Silas; distributed across the three by nature and Service.
  2. Highest open leverageThe Movement-I trigger that makes Amoz's absence undeniable — and does it implicate the sisters' complacency?
  3. HighMaisel's memory rules — what fractures, what holds, and does the pattern map the threat?
  4. Defines series registerThe Movement-III reunion cliffhanger — found-and-changed, found-and-chosen, or fate-understood?
  5. Shapes Maisel through book twoDoes Omin read Maisel's withholding as betrayal, recognition, or both?
  6. Reader sympathy timingIs Omin's distance-as-protection learned early (sympathetic from the start) or discovered (recontextualizing her coldness)?

Start with Movement III. If the convergence holds, everything else will.
